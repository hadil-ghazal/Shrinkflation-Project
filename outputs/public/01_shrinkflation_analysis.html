Shrinkflation Measured: What Smaller Product sizing Actually Did to Prices¶

This project uses research data from the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) to explore shrinkflation which is the practice of reducing product sizes while keeping prices the same and unchanged.

**Why both datasets? Both datasets are meanth to depict different dat elements such as: **

  • The R-CPI-SC Index data shows what inflation would look like if package sizes had never changed.
  • The Frequency File data is counting how often downsizing(decreased size of product for the same cost) and upsizing(increased size of the product for the same cost) occurred in the CPI sample.

By looking at these together, we can see both sides of the shrinkflation coin:

  • How prices moved without shrinkflation’s effect
  • How often shrinkflation events actually happened

Additional Background: Our goal is to communicate a clear data-driven story about how shrinkflation shows up in the BLS data and what it means for consumers. Our main question we are focusing on is How much do package size changes matter overall and are there patterns happening in specific grocery categories over others?

Steps: We will be doing the following:

  1. Loading public BLS data which is capturing size changes and price changes
  2. Clean & reshape ensuring good data hygiede and converting the data into analysis ready versions.
  3. Engineering features will be using rolling totals and rebased indexes
  4. Visualize: We will be creating visuals for the following:
    • Frequency of shrinkflation over time
    • Price changes without shrinkflation
    • Categories most affected
  5. Analysis: we will be analyzing the results and creating commentary on the conclusions. We will also be describing the limitations and the nature of the data.

This project connects messy Excel files, clean datasets, clear visuals. It focuses on building a reproducible narrative about where shrinkflation matters most and how it shapes consumer experience.

Data Sources¶

This project has two main datasets published by the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics it is using:

  • R-CPI-SC Index
    A research version of the Consumer Price Index that removes the effect of package size changes.
    It shows what inflation would look like if products had kept their original sizes.

  • Frequency of Size Changes File
    Monthly counts of how often products in the CPI sample were recorded as downsized (meaning a smaller size of the product for same price) or upsized (meaning larger size product for same price)

Both datasets are monthly and spanning 2015 through the most recent release from a few months ago. They are provided in wide Excel format, so our first step is to reshape them into clean tables with standardized column names and dates.

What the Raw Files Look Like¶

Before cleaning, the BLS files are not analysis-ready:

  • The index workbook contains two sheets (Food at home/grocery ingredients and other categories) with two metadata rows on top that need to be skipped.
  • The frequency workbook has its column headers embedded in the first data row, rather than as a proper header.
  • Both files are in wide format with one column per month instead of organized rows.

To prepare them for analysis the following will be done:

  • Extract the key fields (item, series, date, value)
  • Reshape the monthly columns into a single date column
  • Standardize the names for consistency purposes

This guarantees that both of the datasets can be merged, visualized and analyzed in a straightforward way.

Cleaned Frequency of Size Changes¶

The frequency file tracks how often products in the CPI sample are recorded as being downsized (decreased size for same price) or upsized (increased size for same price).

So after cleaning:

  • Each row contains a date, the monthly downsizing count, and the total upsizing counts.
  • We also calculate the 12-month rolling totals (down_roll/ up_roll) to smooth out short term noise and showcase the long term trends

This dataset shows the activity of shrinkflation over time, not just how much prices changed, but how often packages were changing size in the CPI sample.

# Adding the 12 month total sums

freq_std["up_roll"]   = freq_std["upsizing_total"].rolling(12).sum()
freq_std["net_roll"]  = freq_std["down_roll"] - freq_std["up_roll"]

def load_rcpi_sheet(xlsx_path, sheet):
    """Load one R-CPI-SC sheet: skip top 2 metadata rows, melt wide→long."""
    df = pd.read_excel(xlsx_path, sheet_name=sheet, header=2)
    # drop rows where first is null
    df = df.dropna(subset=[df.columns[0]]).copy()
    # renames first two columns
    df = df.rename(columns={df.columns[0]: "item", df.columns[1]: "series"})
    #  all date columns into long form
    long = df.melt(id_vars=["item","series"], var_name="date", value_name="value")
    # parse month year like January 2015
    long["date"] = pd.to_datetime(long["date"].astype(str), format="%B %Y", errors="coerce")
    # keep only the valid rows
    long = long.dropna(subset=["date","value"])
    return long

How Often Does Shrinkflation Happen?¶

This line chart shows 12-month rolling totals of the downsizing/shrinking (which is in blue) versus the upsizing (which is orange) events in the CPI sample.

  • What we see: Downsizing spiked up during 2021–2022 and peaked at around 74 events in mid2022. While upsizing stayed much lower (it was about 30–35 events)
  • Latest trends: As of June 2025, downsizing/shrinking remains elevated (about 50 events) compared to upsizing (only about ~20 events).
  • Why it matters: These spikes in downsizing happened in the same period when grocery prices were climbing the fastest. That means consumers were experiencing not only higher prices, but also more frequent package reductions at the same time.

This chart is the link between the two stories:

  • The R-CPI-SC charts shows what prices looked like without shrinkflation,
  • The frequency chart shows how often shrinkflation was happening,
  • Together, they explain why shoppers felt a stronger pinch than prices alone can capture.

Note: These counts come from the CPI sample, not the entire marketplace . they are indicative not exhaustive.

Which Categories Feel Shrinkflation the Most?¶

Shrinkflation doesn’t hit every grocery aisle or product equally. Some categories have faced steeper price increases than others. But at the same time, many of these categories also show higher rates of downsizing in the CPI sample.

To highlight where the impact is strongest, we can compare the cumulative price change since December 2019 across diffeent categories using the shrinkflation-adjusted index (R-CPI-SC)

  • This tells us where prices rose the most without shrinkflation baked in.
  • When combined with the frequency data, it helps reveal the aisles where shoppers felt a double effect: not only were prices climbing faster but packages were shrinking more often as well.

The bar chart that follows focuses on this combined phenomena: where grocery inflation has been steepest and where shrinkflation is most visible.

Food at Home: Shrinkflation in Groceries and the Clear Change Since the Pandemic¶

This chart shows the Research CPI without product size changes (R-CPI-SC) for Food at home, rebased to December 2019 = 100.

  • Why Dec 2019? It provides a clean pre-pandemic baseline, making it easy to measure changes in the COVID and post-COVID era.
  • What we see:
    • Prices for groceries at home surged through 2021–2022, reaching a pandemic-era peak of about +28.7% above Dec 2019.
    • As of the latest data point (June 2025), prices remain at this peak level; meaning there has been no meaningful pullback.
  • Why it matters: These increases matter because they show price inflation without shrinkflation included. But when paired with the frequency data, we see that the same period also experienced a surge in downsizing events.
    Together, this means consumers were hit with a double effect:
    1. Higher grocery prices
    2. Smaller packages showing up more often on store shelves.

This figure highlights the felt experience of shrinkflation: even if official inflation slows, elevated prices combined with frequent downsizing create lasting sticker shock for shoppers.
